Protecting Grassland Birds

October 29, 2013 | Tags: CLC,Headline | birds,easements,Ooms,pca

Bobolink – Photo by Andrea Westmoreland

The Bobolinks have returned to Columbia County! Starting in the Southern Hemisphere, it’s a 6,000 mile flight. It’s a spectacular bird with a bubbling song and unique plumage. The male bobolink is the only North American bird that is black underneath and white on the back. After breeding, his plumage changes into a muted and camouflaged appearance.

Over the last thirty years, bobolinks – as well as other grassland birds such as savannah sparrows, grasshopper sparrows, and Eastern meadowlarks – have significantly decreased due to loss of farmland and intensification of certain agricultural practices. Yet, we still have sizable populations and during the warmer months many grassland birds call Columbia County home and take advantage of the numerous meadows and agricultural lands.

HABITAT CONSERVATION

Protecting grassland bird habitat is an important part of CLC’s mission. To date, CLC has permanently protected over 29,400 acres of land and much of this land consists of open fields including farm fields that provide grassland bird habitat. CLC also works with all levels of government to advocate for and provide support for habitat conservation. For example, CLC has designed a proposal and is providing support to transform the site of the former City of Hudson landfill and surrounding open space into a public recreation area that simultaneously protects ecologically valuable grasslands. CLC also promotes and supports municipal-level Conservation Advisory Councils that advocate for habitat protection on a local level.

MOWING SCHEDULE

Conserving grassland bird habitat requires an appropriate mowing schedule. Mowing a field during the nesting and fledging cycle destroys nests and significantly lowers young birds’ chances of survival. However, occasional mowing is required in order to prevent the meadow from transitioning into forest.

The USDA Natural Resources Conservation Services field offices in Vermont and Massachusetts developed a mowing schedule based on research done at the University of Vermont that helps harmonize the needs of farmers and grassland birds. The farmer has two options which allow for variations in weather and growing patterns that will ensure at least one successful breeding and nesting cycle.

• Conduct a cutting on or before May 31, and then not again for at least 65 days, after that point the farmers can safely take additional cuttings of hay
• If the first cutting cannot be done by May 31, then the fields should not be mowed until after mid-July.

CLC’s Ooms Conservation Area at Sutherland Pond is managed to support agriculture and bird habitat and we follow the USDA guidelines. This year, Ooms is scheduled to be mowed on or before May 31 and the cuttings will be hayed and used at A. Ooms & Sons farm.

At other Public Conservation Areas, where active agriculture is not being conducted, smaller fields are mowed annually in late autumn after nesting and fledging cycles. Larger fields are usually mowed on a three year rotational basis. Allowing the fields to remain completely uncut has additional habitat benefits for other species including butterflies, hawks, and certain mammals.

Protecting Columbia County wildlife habitat requires a broad based effort. We commend the Columbia County Department of Public Works for adjusting their mowing schedule of the Hudson North Bay to conform to the USDA guidelines. Columbia Land Conservancy (and the bobolinks) are also grateful to the landowners, donors, and volunteers who support CLC’s efforts and make our conservation successes possible.
