Farming: Our Future?

February 27, 2013 | Tags: Land Matters,Publication,Working Farms

by Peter Paden
Published March 1, 2013 in the Register-Star

On Saturday, February 23rd, more than 200 people gathered together at the Taconic Hills High School for a day of learning, discussion and networking focused on the future of farming in Columbia County. The second annual conference entitled: Farming Our Future – Growing Food, Farms and Community brought together a diverse array of folks – experienced farmers, aspiring farmers, people who own farm land or are looking to acquire it, professionals who work in farm-related businesses and non-profits, professionals from the food industry, educators, political and community leaders, and people who love living in a rural area and want to ensure it will remain so.

It was a terrific event. The program featured an informative and inspirational keynote speech by Jim Slama, a national leader in the local food movement, and an opportunity to attend workshops focusing on opportunities and challenges associated with the explosion in recent years of demand for high quality, locally grown food. In between these offerings participants were able to enjoy a delicious sampling of Columbia County fare while making contacts and connections with old friends and new acquaintances around a shared interest in the multi-faceted potential of the resurgent farm and food industries.

Good Food = Good Business

Slama is the founder of Chicago-based FamilyFarmed.org, which encourages the production, marketing, and distribution of locally grown and responsibly produced food. Slama argues convincingly that a major evolution is underway in our society’s values concerning the food we eat. Widespread awareness of issues relating to human health, the environment, and the economy has led to an enormous demand for what Slama calls “good food,” which he defines as “food that is grown and processed as close to the point of consumption as possible using sustainable, humane and fair practices.” He reports that according to the National Restaurant Association, for the past five years the number one concern of American diners has been a desire for locally grown food. An array of statistics substantiates the point and there is every reason to believe that the trend has not come close to peaking. (A recent study of demand for local food in New York City suggested that the roughly $100 million in current sales could easily grow seven-fold.)

Based on facts such as these, Slama has coined the powerful slogan “good food = good business.” He’s saying that there will be a tremendous market for goods and services produced by anyone who can successfully plug into the good food chain and contribute to meeting the demand for these products. In other words, the good food phenomenon has created a fabulous array of business opportunities. He goes on, however, to note that the vast majority of food consumed in America is not locally sourced. There are a number of reasons for this which present challenges that must be overcome if the demand for good food is to reach its full potential. These challenges include an insufficient number of farmers, the high cost of farm land, lack of local agricultural infrastructure, lack of sufficient aggregation and distribution facilities and very important food safety concerns. But Slama argues that all of these challenges can be met, and if we successfully do so, our society and our culture will be markedly changed for the better.

Practical Follow-ups

Energized and inspired by Slama’s presentation, conference attendees broke up into smaller groups to explore a number of concrete, practical challenges associated with building a good food future. Discussions led by experienced professionals centered on food distribution, access to farm land, sources of financing, creating farm-friendly communities and how individuals who care about these issues can constructively become involved with them. There was a high degree of interest and engagement in these sessions, and the great cross-section of conference attendees made for discussions that were particularly rich and informative.

A Timely Conversation

The conference could not have been held at a more auspicious moment for Columbia County. As I noted in last month’s column, the Board of Supervisors will soon be asked to vote on a matter of great consequence: whether or not to adopt a Columbia County Agriculture and Farmland Protection Plan. The proposed Plan lays out a four-pronged strategy “to conserve and manage the exceptionally rich agricultural resources of Columbia County and ensure that farming continues to play a central role in the economy and culture of our communities.” Adoption of the Plan, by itself, will not insure that any of the desired benefits will materialize. However, it will represent a commitment on the part of county leadership to make agriculture a central component of our economic development strategy and of our vision for the character of our landscape and our communities far into the future. (A final public meeting to discuss and receive input on the draft Plan will be held at the Ancram Town Hall on Saturday March 2, from 10AM until noon.)

“Good Food = Good Business” – that would be a perfect tag line for the economic development strategy embodied in the proposal Farmland Protection Plan. I doubt there is any community in the state that is better positioned to capitalize on the nation’s evolving tastes in food than Columbia County. Situated within three hours of more than 50 million consumers, encompassing well over 100,000 acres of good farmland, the county enjoys a rich agricultural heritage and a substantial base of agriculture-related infrastructure. Many of our families have for years, if not generations, been actively engaged with farming the land. Hundreds of entrepreneurial young people have come here, or are thinking about it, seeking opportunities to get involved with farming. And the vast majority of people who live here highly value the rural character and heritage of the area and want to retain it.

Our great farmland is Columbia County’s metaphorical oil field. It’s our primary exploitable natural resource. If we are smart, we will take advantage of our resources and the nation’s rapidly evolving food culture to build a prosperous local agricultural economy. Such an economy will encourage the growth of compatible sectors, such as tourism, outdoor recreation and any business that can function productively in a rural setting. All of this will ensure that the qualities that make Columbia County such a special place, our extensive forests and open spaces, vast water resources, rich wildlife habitats and scenic vistas, and our historic rural patterns of residential development, will remain long into the future.

Thanks to the Conference Organizers

The conference was organized by an ad hoc steering committee of individuals working in various ways to support and promote the county’s farm sector. Co-chairs were Karen DiPeri and Steve Hadcock. Organizations represented on the steering committee were Cornell Cooperative Extension, Columbia Land Conservancy, Hawthorne Valley Association, Hudson Valley Agribusiness Development Corporation, Taconic Hills Central School District, and Farm Credit East.

They did a wonderful job! It was especially wonderful – and unusual in my experience – for several hundred people from widely varying sectors of the community to get together and focus in a substantive, constructive and friendly way on a major issue confronting our county. I hope we will see much more of this kind of county-wide community and dialogue in months and years to come.

Peter Paden is Executive Director of the Columbia Land Conservancy. His column appears on the first Friday of every month.
